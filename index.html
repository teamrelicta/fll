Problem Statement

Ice-patch archaeology is extremely fragile because artifacts can break, decay, or melt as soon as they‚Äôre exposed. Rapid climate warming creates unpredictable freeze‚Äìthaw cycles, meaning artifacts may appear one year and disappear the next. Archaeologists have only a short window to locate, document, and safely extract items before they degrade or the ice patch melts further. This makes continuous monitoring and careful preservation essential.
üìö

Research

Ice patches provide a wide variety of artifacts such as organic materials, human artifacts, environmental clues. Unlike other archeological sites, ice patches can bring more recent objects to the same level as older ones by intermittently melting. Scientists will usually monitor the site for Ice depth and temperature variations using tools like GPR, LIDAR.
‚ú®

Our Solution

Our robot scans the ice patch using dual-frequency antennas, LiDAR, and thermal imaging to locate buried artifacts with high accuracy. Once confirmed, it loosens the surrounding ice using cold air, micro-vibrations, and controlled regelation-based micro-heating. A precision robotic arm gently lifts the artifact and places it into a cold preservation capsule. The entire process protects the site, prevents melting, and keeps the artifact stable for archaeologists.
üîß

Prototypes & Testing

Team RELICTA created a rough Arduino-based prototype to test the core ideas behind our Ice Patch Archaeology solution. The prototype was designed to explore how a robot can move carefully, locate objects precisely, and handle fragile artifacts without causing damage. Our model uses an Arduino controller, servo motors, and a robotic arm to simulate slow navigation over an ice patch and delicate extraction of artifacts. We conducted multiple tests to evaluate movement stability, arm control, and safe handling using lightweight mock artifacts. During testing, we adjusted motor speeds to reduce vibration, limited servo angles to prevent sudden movements, and improved the arm‚Äôs stability. We also included an emergency stop to ensure safety during operation. Through this process, we learned that precision is more important than speed, and that small design changes can greatly improve safety. Our prototype demonstrates that robotic assistance can support archaeologists by reducing human risk while protecting fragile cultural heritage.
